Introduction
As our education system moves to a more personalized style of education in the 21st century, the importance and effectiveness of project-based learning has been recognized by more and more people. However, as the popularity of PBL grows, there are still some problems to be solved. After a 3-round user research, we identified our problem space with several essential experience models. With storyboards, Speed Dating with our target client and learner experience map, our ideation and design process become very solid.

This project has be funded by NSF. We are pushing it forward and try our best to make it come true.

Project-based learning (PBL) is a student-centered pedagogy that involves a dynamic classroom approach aimed at helping students acquire a deeper knowledge through active exploration of real-world challenges. The direct output of a PBL course is usually a project with the goal of solving some problems. In many courses, these projects are done collaboratively in groups, though sometimes it is also an option for students to work independently.
01 Problem Space
In project-based learning, students generally come in to the course with some idea of what they hope to learn, their “learning goals.” The teacher might have some specific learning goals that they’d like all students to work toward as well, such as “collaboration skills” in a group-based project. However, often times each group will be working toward different learning goals that relate to their own project, for example in the CS Innovations course we observed one student wanted to learn about machine learning, while another was learning how to program for VR. Sometimes students get bogged down in small tasks or problems or become distracted and lose sight of their course learning goals in PBL courses. It also sometimes difficult for teachers to assess whether students are meeting their learning goals in order to provide useful feedbacks and, at the end of the course, fair grades.
Learning goals setting and tracking
It is not always easy for students to identify their learning goals and keep them in mind while working on group projects.
Collaboration
Unbalanced share of work among team members.
Assessment
Teachers find it difficult to assess various groups that are working on different projects.
02 Solution
We aim to address the problem of losing track of one’s learning goals with our GoTracker system. This system enables students to set their learning goals at the beginning of the course and to track their progress toward these goals over the duration of the project by setting weekly tasks and capturing daily progress. It will also allow teachers to see students’ progress towards these goals, approving or disapproving their goal - evidence alignment, which will be later used to generate the progress bar. The proposed system will be described in more detail in later chapters.
With our system, students will be better able to keep track of their learning goal progress, which we believe will lead to increased effort on task completion, decreased frustration in facing failures, and a sense of fairness in viewing assessment. It could increase time on task (as opposed to spent on off-task behavior) because if a student becomes unsure of what to do next, they can look back at their learning goals to help them determine a next step that will allow them to make progress toward one or more of those goals. It will hopefully decrease frustration as it will help students focus on the bigger picture rather than be disappointed by small failures. A sense of fairness in assessment will come out of the students’ ability to see how much progress they made toward their learning goals over the semester, as well as teacher feedback on task completion week-to-week, which will help make the final grading more transparent.
Key Features
Whole-package PBL
Our system assists students along the project making process, including project proposal, students' self-defined learning goals, setting weekly tasks, making daily progress, making achievement on learning goals, self-reflection and final grading.
Plan Your Project
Students need to make weekly plan for their project. And they will add daily progress during the week based on weekly tasks of the current week. They can also review weekly tasks completion of previous weeks.
Focus on Learning
Each piece of daily progress is aligned with specific learning goals, which help students put more emphasis on the achievement of learning goals they really care about. In this case, failures along project making would not beat their passion and interest easily because our system bring back the nature of project-based learning.

04 Research & Synthesis
Understanding Stakeholders’ Perspectives and Needs
Interviews with Stakeholders
For this round of interviews, we generated different questions based on different stakeholders’ perspectives, including a specific PBL course Research Science, the PBL courses in City as Our Campus, school’s overall opinion on project-based learning and pros and cons of PBL courses. For each interview, one person acted as the interviewer, taking the lead on asking questions, one person took notes, and a third recorded the audio and took photographs.
Diagnostic Map
Next, we created a diagnostic map (Jesper Simonsen and Karin Friberg, 2014) to detect existing problems in project-based learning courses at Winchester Thurston. Problems, their potential causes and consequences were identified as reported by the stakeholders through our affinity diagram and user profile boards. Then we generated ideas for how these problems could be solved (including both ideas of potential pieces to include in our design and other possible solutions that might not be things we could actually implement).
The problems were broken down into four categories - assessment, project-making, collaboration, and others. We first created a map together using sticky notes on a whiteboard and then converted it to a digital version for readability.

Understanding Learners in Context
After discussing the goals and challenges of project-based learning at Winchester Thurston with the stakeholders above, we wanted to validate our findings from the students' perspective, so we did a combined classroom observation and survey study to gather this data.
Data Consolidation
We then created an affinity diagram to further synthesize the qualitative data from the survey with the observations from the classroom.

Based on our affinity diagram, we found that students tended to fall into two overarching types, “collaborators” and “individualists.” We created identity models, a type of model which shows “the aspects of users’ personal identity that matter to [the] design” (Holtzblatt & Beyer 2016a), for each of these personality types.

In the open-response question about what value students got from PBL courses, responses also included “learning to slowly work on a long term project,” “time management and how to pace long term projects,” and “ the responsibility of completing a big project with less deadlines and hand holding from your instructor.” This take-away of time management skills was one we had not thought to ask about originally and it was interesting to see that students reported that they felt it was a valuable benefit of PBL.

Synthesis of Research Findings
Synthesis of the Data
After conducting and analyzing three user studies, we found that a student’s success and ability to participate in project-based learning depends on many people- not only the student and instructor, but also community mentors, parents, and peers, as well as those who might be evaluating their transcripts for college admissions.

We decided to analyze the influence of these roles from the view of the student, since project-based learning is student-centered. We categorized other roles according to their level of intimacy with the students in the context of project-based learning. Therefore, we decided to create a relationship model (Holtzblatt & Beyer, 2016b).

Ideation
STEP 1 | Initial Storyboards

Each of us on the team brainstormed 5 ideas for the solutions to our problem space and made 3-panel experience storyboards for them.

STEP 2 | Speed Dating

Through group discussion, 10 storyboards out of 20 in total were selected for the purpose of a speed dating (Zimmerman & Forlizzi, 2017) with our client. After the speed dating, a comprehensive design solution was made, which is the combination of three of our original storyboards.

STEP 3 | Final Decision

However, the integrated storyboard including three ideas became too complicated and the system was overwhelming and confusing. So we decided to keep two ideas to focus on in our final design solution, the following prototyping and development stages.

Final Storyboards Selected
Learner Experience Map
Based on insights from speed dating, we hope to design a system that can help students have a clearer understanding of their learning goals. Using this system, students can design their own learning goals with scaffolding from the instructor, and track how much progress they’ve made toward them so far, and where they can improve. They can receive timely and targeted feedback from their teacher, at both the group and individual level. Teachers will also be better able to track their students’ progress toward their learning goals and more easily assess their final outcomes.

Additionally, this system will help the teacher with final grading, as the teacher can use the system’s progress bars, in tandem with students’ final self-reflections, to assist in assigning grades at the end of the course. We believe that this system can reduce frustration in both students and teachers of PBL courses.

Design & Prototype
Static Prototype
User Test 1 for Role
With the static prototypes and questions we want to answer, we did the first round of user test with the high school students from the PBL course Computer Science Innovation in Winchester Thurston School, as well as a new round of class observation, stakeholder interview and speed dating.

The primary objective of this prototype is to test the role (Hounde & Hill, 1997) of our design solution in a PBL course.

Role (Primary)

  • Does the work flow of the system (esp. self-set learning goals) match with the students’ work flow in the course?
  • What values do students think the system can bring them?
  • How fair/trustworthy do the students feel the system is for the assessment?
  • How much burden do students feel when using the system?

Look & Feel (Secondary)

  • Is the interaction of the system intuitive for students?

User Test 1 - Findings
Value - Match with the course although it’s individual project
  • “Everyone does a different project and has different learning goals for this course”
  • “We set our own goals and discuss them with the teacher ”

Value - Keep track of what students have learned

  • “Most helpful to look backward at what I learned, I want to look back on this.”

Burden - Not much burden

  • “I don’t think it’s a lot burden to me. I think uploading the work I did and keeping record of them is very helpful. I want to put more details on what I did every day.”

Fairness

  • Students trust the way the teacher is currently grading them using the agile framework.

Interaction

  • Overall Intuitive
  • Room for improvement in some features
    • “What does ‘task’ mean in in the daily log interface? Can you specify that?”
    • “I can only upload the work if I am one of the collaborators? What if my teammate asks me to upload for him when he’s away and I’m not one of the collaborators?”
Interactive Prototype & Parallel Design
User Test 1 for Look & Feel
With the dynamic prototype, we did the second round of user test with 2 of the students in the CS Innovation course. We asked them to think aloud while interacting with the prototype and completing some tasks including upload the design proposal, set the learning goals, check teacher’s feedbacks, set weekly tasks, upload daily progress, check the progress for each learning goal, and so on. We also did an A/B testing with the two designs for setting weekly tasks and entering the daily logs interface.

Look & Feel

  • Is the interface intuitive and user friendly?
  • Are there any parts that are confusing or redundant?
  • Parallel designs: do you have a preference?
  • Could this replace the current agile system they are using in the class?

User Test 2 - Findings
Overall interaction - Intuitive
  • In most cases, users can complete the tasks we listed smoothly.
  • “It’s pretty intuitive”

Confusions - Some features

  • Sidebar and header
  • What is the sequence of the features on home page
  • How the progress bar is generated
  • What is the definition of daily log

Parallel design - Version B is preferable

  • “It makes more sense to add logs based on weekly tasks.”
  • The icons need to be more intuitive.

Improve current confusing interfaces

  • Refine the navigation in sidebar and header
  • Add arrow to show sequence for each step on the homepage
  • Change the term “Daily Log” to “Daily Progress”
  • More obvious “Add daily Progress” button in the daily progress page
  • Add “completion proportion” when uploading daily progress
  • Add “Explanation” for the progress bar

Iterate Version B

  • Users can set weekly tasks and subtasks
  • Add entrance of daily log in the weekly task page
  • Refine the icons in version B
